Make your own Logo

In this project, you will create your own personal logo.

A logo is a a symbol or other design adopted by an organization to identify its products, uniform, vehicles, etc.

As you can imagine, a logo is crucial to a company, but it is also the first design that a client sees featuring your brand.


My name is Melina Sapiano, and this is my personal logo

As many GraphicDesigners do, I freelance. I have used this logo for my self-published book of concept and game art. My logo, while not a standard GraphicDesigner's logo is unique to my work and style. Clients are able to see my distinct logo and expect a certain style from me. In turn, you will do this as well.


Criteria

The criterion for the logo design project is as follows:

  1. You are tasked to create a logo design that represents you as a person. Your logo should represent/symbolize your personal interests and beliefs. A good way to start is think about what you love, what you like to do, or something you think is really interesting that resonates with you.
  2. Follow the logo design process (provided below).
  3. Use up to three colors (no more) within the design: keep it simple, not too visually distracting.
  4. Create an initial sketch of the logo.
  5. Create a final computer GraphicDesign of your logo.

Step 1: Research your logo

Before you start designing, you’ll need to do research

Research will make you a better designer, saving you a lot of thinking time. This step is crucial. You'll become more engaged with the client's needs, making your design far superior than the rest. Don't skip this step if you're entering a freelance design contest, as some time spent on research (up to 30 mins) will give you an advantage.


Look at:

  1. Understand the business: look at other GraphicDesigner's logos. Go ahead and Google other GraphicDesigners!
  2. Target demographic (age, gender, location). Think about who would commission you? Who are your clients?
  3. History & culture of the brand. While you are just starting as a GraphicDesigner, you need to understand the culture you are now a part of.

Step 2: Brainstorming and Sketch

Now is the time to pull all your ideas together.

Do this part by hand. Do not open GIMP yet


Get a piece of paper and pen or pencil. Do a few small, quarter sized examples. These are called thumbnails. Thumbnails are used by GraphicDesigners and photographers for a small image representation of a larger images, usually intended to make it easier and faster to look at or manage a group of larger images.

These do not need to be perfect representations. You should do 3-5 quick thumbnails. They do not need to be ornately details, just clear enough for you to understand the premise.

Start by creating symbols or motifs. Explore and test typefaces and keep in mind the latest logo and GraphicDesign trends when you're working. Make sure to jot all your ideas down.

Thumbnails are an irreplaceable part of the design process. It may seem trivial when you have a very vivid idea, but they are a great starting point. Blocking a few out at a time helps piece together ideas and can fix big problems before you put too much work in. It’s a good idea to do 4 thumbnails to start, or if you get stuck. Thumbnails are immeasurably useful, and even the most advanced GraphicDesigners do them.

Step 3: Build

Now, it's time to jump into GIMP. Go ahead and open it on your task bar.

Watch this video, following along in GIMP

Now that you are well aquainted with the innerworkings of GIMP, you will begin to build your logo.

This process will take time. GIMP is a large program with a lot of functionality. It will take a while for this program to become second nature, but now is the time to start!

Step 4: Review

Part of Designing is critique. After you think, sketch, labor over your piece, and finish it, how do you know it’s good? This is when you need to connect with your peers, and discuss each other’s pieces. This video will open you to the world of designing critiques.

The critique is a fundamental part of the design process. This helps you discuss ideas out with your peers and instructors. Be prepared for criticism. While it is hard to hear that your design didn't cut the mustard, listen to your responses and push your design to be even better


It's time to get feedback

Choose two fellow peers. Ask them for honest feedback on your logo. Ask them what they like. What they don't like. Take notes, and ask what they would change.

Reach out to an instructor

Understandably, your fellow peers might not be working on the same course work you are. They might not know anything about GraphicDesign, but this is where your instructor can help

Ask one of your instructors to critique your design. Set up a time to meet and get back together to chat about your logo. Somtimes during class, is not the best time to critique. You will get the best advice by allowing the instructor to mull on it and give you a solid review of your work.

Step 5: Revise

After your review, you have changes to make.

Every design has revisions and changes. No one gets it right the first time, not even the strongest GraphicDesigners. Critiques and changes to a design are normal. Clients often see their idea fleshed out, and it's not always the first draft.

Critiques are a common occurance for the professional GraphicDesigner, and often saught after by freelancers.


Revisions do not mean you fail, they mean you now know how to succeed.
