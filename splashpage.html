For this assignment, you have been tasked with designing an above-the-fold landing page for a startup's website.

A landing page is the first page that a visitor sees when arriving on a company’s website.

Since first impressions are crucial online (and since most visitors will only spend a moment or two before deciding whether to invest additional time learning about a product), a strong landing is a crucial factor in a startup’s growth.

A local startup is holding a contest for their website. They organize and host board game parties. They are looking for a branding identity including a logo and color pallete, a short description of their services, and a clear call to action to compel the visitor to continue learning more or to sign up.

Applying the UI/UX

Start Wireframing

Remembering wireframing from the Grids lesson, Wireframing is an important part of designing a website for a client or for yourself.

Logo Design

You have been creating a lot of logos, though don't forget a step.

Get out a piece of paper and pen/pencil, start to sketch out an idea for the company. Start to think of relevant ideas to the company for the logo.

Would a chess piece work? Or are there other, broader ideas?

Open GIMP and start the logo design

Use https://coolors.co/app to pick your colour schemes.

Start to build the website with html, start to think about hierarchy, and flow, and image placement.

Don't forget to look for stock images through Canva

Review

The critique is a fundamental part of the design process. This helps you discuss ideas out with your peers and instructors. Be prepared for criticism. While it is hard to hear that your design didn't cut the mustard, listen to your responses and push your design to be even better


It's time to get feedback

Choose two fellow peers. Ask them for honest feedback on your logo. Ask them what they like. What they don't like. Take notes, and ask what they would change.

Reach out to an instructor

Understandably, your fellow peers might not be working on the same course work you are. They might not know anything about Graphic Design, but this is where your instructor can help

Ask one of your instructors to critique your design. Set up a time to meet and get back together to chat about your logo. Somtimes during class, is not the best time to critique. You will get the best advice by allowing the instructor to mull on it and give you a solid review of your work.

Final Step: Revise

After your review, you have changes to make.

Every design has revisions and changes. No one gets it right the first time, not even the strongest Graphic Designers. Critiques and changes to a design are normal. Clients often see their idea fleshed out, and it's not always the first draft.

Critiques are a common occurance for the professional Graphic Designer, and often saught after by freelancers.
