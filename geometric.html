Let's get started

Step 1: Choose Your Image

For the purposes of this Instructable, I'm going to cut out the main subject, and make the background a solid color. Doing so makes it less confusing to the viewer, and brings the attention to the subject.

You should choose a relatively high-quality photo (if you can see the individual pixels, that's not usually a good thing). Making sure your subject has multiple colors, or shades of colors, is important, which you'll see why later on. You'll also want a pretty large subject (don't choose a person standing at the edge of the frame in the distance, for example). Faces turn out extremely cool looking with this edit, so that's what I'm going to use.

Look for Stock photos from Canva

Step 2: Open Your Image in Gimp

Open the application, then go in the top left corner, select "File" then "Open". A box will pop up, now you can select your photo.

Step 3: The Tools

The main tools we will be using will be the tool and the eye dropper tool.

Step 4: Lasso It!

Use the lasso tool to "lasso" a little triangle.

Step 5: Select Your Color

Use the eyedropper tool to select the main color in the area that you just selected with the lasso. This now becomes the foreground color.

Step 6: Fill It With the Color

Now fill that little triangle with the color you just selected. What you're actually doing is filling the area with the foreground color (which is what you just selected using the eyedropper tool), which you can also do under the edit tab.

Step 7: Repeat!

Now fill that little triangle with the color you just selected. What you're actually doing is filling the area with the foreground color (which is what you just selected using the eyedropper tool), which you can also do under the edit tab.

Step 8: Cut Out the Whole Image

Finally done with your triangles? Well, you're not done yet! Just a few more steps. To cut out the image you just made, right click on the image's little bar in the box on the right hand side. That doesn't make sense but I don't know what it's called, so whatever. Just look at the picture. Click Add Alpha Channel. If it somehow already has one added, great. This adds a layer of translucency to the image.

Step 9: Select the Whole Image

Now take your lasso tool, and carefully select the outside of the entire image.

Step 10: Selection

Now you need to go into the Select option in the top right hand corner, and choose Invert.

Step 11: Actually Cut It Out

Now cut out the area around your image, leaving just your subject.

Step 12: Make Your Background!

This is the fun part: making the background!

First, choose your background color. Look down at the bottom of the toolbox (the thing on the left). You will see whatever color you last used. Click the color UNDER it (which should be black or white) and choose the color you want for the background. You'll need to add a layer in the box on the right hand side, click that little folder. Then choose background color under the Layer Fill Type section and click ok.

Step 13: Put It in the Back

Click and drag it behind your main image

Review

The critique is a fundamental part of the design process. This helps you discuss ideas out with your peers and instructors. Be prepared for criticism. While it is hard to hear that your design didn't cut the mustard, listen to your responses and push your design to be even better


It's time to get feedback

Choose two fellow peers. Ask them for honest feedback on your logo. Ask them what they like. What they don't like. Take notes, and ask what they would change.

Reach out to an instructor

Understandably, your fellow peers might not be working on the same course work you are. They might not know anything about Graphic Design, but this is where your instructor can help

Ask one of your instructors to critique your design. Set up a time to meet and get back together to chat about your logo. Somtimes during class, is not the best time to critique. You will get the best advice by allowing the instructor to mull on it and give you a solid review of your work.

Final Step: Revise

After your review, you have changes to make.

Every design has revisions and changes. No one gets it right the first time, not even the strongest Graphic Designers. Critiques and changes to a design are normal. Clients often see their idea fleshed out, and it's not always the first draft.

Critiques are a common occurance for the professional Graphic Designer, and often saught after by freelancers.
